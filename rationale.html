Pedagogical Rationale

Why Alignment Matters — and How We Have Made It Work

The theoretical and practical case for our AoL approach, grounded in constructive alignment and designed to benefit students, staff, and the quality of our programs.

Foundation: Constructive Alignment as Standard Practice

The Adelaide Business School's Assurance of Learning strategy is grounded in constructive alignment (Biggs, 1996; Biggs and Tang, 2011), the principle that intended learning outcomes, teaching and learning activities, and assessment tasks must form a coherent and mutually reinforcing system. What distinguishes the School's approach is not merely that constructive alignment is advocated but that it is operationalised as standard, institution-wide practice — embedded in the way every course is designed, taught, and assessed.

This baseline is achieved through systematic programme mapping: the explicit documentation of how Course Learning Outcomes (CLOs) connect to Programme Learning Outcomes (PLOs), how assessment tasks are aligned to those CLOs, and how the curriculum sequence builds progressively toward graduate-level capability. Kopera-Frye et al. (2008) demonstrate that curriculum mapping of this kind serves a dual purpose — it provides administrators and accreditation bodies with transparent evidence of programme coherence, whilst simultaneously giving academic staff a structured mechanism for identifying gaps and inconsistencies in their own course design. Wijngaards-de Meij and Merx (2018) extend this argument, showing that making the curriculum visible to students through aligned outcomes and assessment criteria improves both learning goal achievement and student understanding of their own development.

This approach to mapping and alignment has been formalised through close collaboration between Teaching and Learning Innovation (TLI) curriculum design experts and Teaching Academics across all disciplines. The School has made a deliberate commitment to a partnership model in which Teaching Academics are supported through the design process rather than directed through it. This collaborative model has been a significant factor in the willingness of staff to engage seriously with alignment as a design discipline rather than an administrative imposition. The result is that Teaching Academics feel genuinely supported in their practice, and grow in confidence that the courses they design and deliver are coherent, purposeful, and in line with best practice in curriculum design.

Note
The AoL strategy described here represents one component within a broader evidence framework. The College also draws on indirect data from student evaluations of teaching and learning and outcomes from Work Integrated Learning placements. This document focuses specifically on the pedagogical rationale underpinning the direct, assignment-based strand of the AoL strategy.

The Quality of Outcomes and AQF Level Progression

A significant consequence of embedding constructive alignment as standard practice — and particularly of requiring Teaching Academics to map rubric criteria explicitly to CLOs — is that it generates sustained and productive attention to the quality of the outcomes themselves. When a Teaching Academic must design an assignment criterion that directly captures a stated CLO, any imprecision, irrelevance, or mismatch in that CLO becomes immediately apparent. This is a productive form of pressure: it guards against what Biggs and Tang (2011) identify as a significant risk in assignment design, where criteria end up measuring skills that are tangential to, rather than constitutive of, the intended learning outcomes — a form of negative backwash in which marks reflect competencies other than those the course was designed to develop.

A further benefit emerges when Teaching Academics consider how each outcome is represented across multiple assignments throughout the course. When a decaying average approach — or any other method of aggregating outcome attainment across assignments — is applied, Teaching Academics must think carefully about what level of the outcome can reasonably be assessed at each point in the course. A complex CLO cannot be fully assessed in an early assignment. This constraint is pedagogically generative: it forces attention to the learning sequence that must be in place for an outcome to be assessed meaningfully at a given point — what must be taught first, what scaffolding is required, and how the demands placed on students should increase as the course progresses.

When Teaching Academics are required to map assignment criteria to outcomes across each assignment in a course, any mismatch between the cognitive demand of the outcome and the year level at which it is taught becomes visible and can be corrected. As outcomes at each level are calibrated appropriately to AQF expectations, a natural progression of learning emerges across the programme as a whole. Graduate qualities — derived from PLOs and representing the culminating capabilities the School commits to developing — are therefore not simply asserted at programme level but demonstrably scaffolded across the years of study.

Beyond Grades: Outcome-Level Precision in Assessment

In conventional assessment practice, the passing of an assignment is treated as evidence that a student has achieved the outcomes associated with that assignment. This assumption is understandable but imprecise. An assignment grade is an aggregate measure, reflecting performance across multiple criteria that may each map to different learning outcomes. It is therefore entirely possible for a student — or indeed the majority of a cohort — to pass an assignment overall whilst performing poorly against the specific criteria that are the primary indicators of a particular outcome. The aggregate grade masks the learning gap entirely: no flag is raised, no intervention is prompted, and the gap is carried forward unaddressed into the next teaching period and, potentially, into the attainment data for the associated PLO. The same dynamic affects students directly — a passing grade offers little incentive to reflect carefully on which aspects of their performance were strong and which were not, diminishing the opportunity for meaningful self-regulation.

Key
The Business School's AoL strategy resolves this limitation by making outcome attainment a distinct and explicit data point, separate from the grade. When a course coordinator designs an assignment rubric, each criterion is mapped to a specific CLO. The Teaching Academic then makes a judgement about CLO attainment based on the aggregated performance across the relevant criteria — not simply from the overall assignment grade.

This process is supported by Canvas, which allows outcomes to be attached directly to rubric criteria. The LMS calculates a Learning Mastery level for each outcome based on the criteria to which it is mapped. Where a CLO is assessed more than once across the course, Canvas supports a decaying average weighting, so that the most recent and typically most demanding assignment carries greater weight in determining the final attainment level. This reflects sound assessment theory: demonstrated capability at the end of a learning sequence is a stronger indicator of genuine achievement than performance earlier in the course (Black and Wiliam, 1998).

The final output for each student is not only a grade profile but also an outcome attainment profile — a record of how each CLO has been achieved, partially achieved, or not achieved. This is visible to students through the Learning Mastery Pathway view in Canvas, enabling meaningful self-regulation and targeted preparation before summative assignments. It is simultaneously available to course coordinators as a cohort-level view, enabling real-time identification of outcomes where students are not progressing as expected — and crucially, the ability to respond whilst the course is still in progress, whether by directing students to additional resources, adjusting tutorial focus, or providing targeted guidance before the next summative task.

A Meaningful Achievement: Teaching Academics Embracing Outcome-Mapped Rubric Design

It would be remiss not to acknowledge what the Business School has achieved in reaching a position where Teaching Academics routinely and confidently map rubric criteria to learning outcomes. When Teaching Academics design rubrics with outcomes in mind from the outset, the alignment process is strengthened at its most fundamental level: the assignment task is designed to capture what the course is intended to develop, not merely to produce a grade. This makes course design more robust, more transparent, and more defensible — to students, to the School, and to accreditation reviewers.

A course coordinator observing that CLO 2 is not well attained — despite acceptable overall grades — can investigate whether the issue lies in how the outcome is taught, how frequently it is assessed, how clearly it is communicated to students, or how the rubric criteria capture it. Interventions can then be targeted and specific. These are precisely the kinds of closing-the-loop actions that meaningful quality assurance requires, and outcome-level data makes them both more actionable and more demonstrably evidence-based.
